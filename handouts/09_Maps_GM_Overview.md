# Megacomplex Maps: GM Overview

## Core idea

The Mercy Twelve scenario area is not a standalone hospital building in an open city.

It is a vertical district inside a vast megacomplex: a city under ceilings, with interior streets, stacked floors, service shafts, clinic rooms, school rooms, shops, maintenance sectors, and emergency landing access.

Think of it as:

```text
roof / landing access
quarantine and incident floor
clinic and emergency floor
community support / shelter floor
utility and service floor
dummy floors: shopping, housing, transit, storage, offices
```

The maps do not need to be literally adjacent in the image files. In play, they represent different floors/sectors in the same vertical stack.

## Movement between maps

The maps include two main vertical travel methods:

### Elevator core

The large dark double-door or teal-lit shaft-like structure is the main elevator core.

Use it for:

- gurneys
- stretchers
- cargo
- civilians
- wheelchair access
- NightCrash’s Gurney Angels
- fast movement between public/medical floors

If the power is unstable, the elevator can become a scene objective.

Possible complications:

- elevator locks down during corporate recovery protocol
- Bluewire or a gang underling holds it open
- corp drones override the lift route
- alien signal interference sends it to the wrong floor
- staff need it for patients, but PCs need it for tactical movement

### Emergency stair / ladderwell

The narrow stair-looking structures are emergency vertical access.

They may look steep or ladder-like on the map. In-world, treat them as compact megacomplex stairwells: steep industrial stairs, ladder-stairs, maintenance stairs, or switchback emergency stairs depending on what fits the scene.

Use them for:

- risky movement under pressure
- stealth movement
- bypassing a locked elevator
- chasing someone between floors
- moving one or two people at a time

Do not use them for easy patient evacuation unless the players have special equipment, enough time, or help from drones.

If a player says, “that looks more like a ladder,” that is fine. It can be a steep maintenance stair/ladderwell. Mechanically, the important point is:

- elevator = safe and accessible but controllable/lockable
- stairs/ladderwell = always available but slower, riskier, and bad for stretchers

## Dummy floors

Between the active maps, assume there are “dummy floors” where no major encounter is planned.

Examples:

- shopping concourse
- food court
- cheap housing stack
- storage mezzanine
- school annex overflow
- transit platform
- maintenance crawl level
- closed office floor
- indoor garden balcony

Use dummy floors for short transitions, rumors, shopping, brief NPC interactions, or pressure from crowds.

## Suggested stack

This is the suggested order:

```text
Floor D: Quarantine / Incident Floor + Roof Landing Corner
Floor A: Mercy Twelve Clinic / Emergency Intake
Floor B: Community Support / Shelter / School Annex
Floor C: Service / Utility / Maintenance Floor
```

Optional dummy floors:

```text
Between D and A: staff offices, diagnostics, closed research suites
Between A and B: public pharmacy, cafeteria, small shops
Between B and C: storage, laundry, staff dorms, low-rent housing
Below C: transit tunnels, waste processing, old sealed sectors
```

## Reveal style

These maps are designed for room-by-room reveal.

Recommended Roll20 approach:

- Use Dynamic Lighting if available.
- Put doors on the Dynamic Lighting layer.
- Keep interior streets visible if they are public/common areas.
- Reveal rooms only when doors are opened or windows/feeds are accessed.
- Let players use cameras, clinic staff, gang guides, or maintenance maps to reveal parts of the map in advance.

The surrounding indoor streets are useful because they allow:

- civilians to pass by
- gang lookouts to loiter without entering the clinic
- corp surveillance to appear at a distance
- PCs to move between sectors
- public consequences to matter
- NightCrash or emergency services to arrive from elsewhere
